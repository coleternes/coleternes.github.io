Building a Bartop Arcade Machine - Part 1

Cole Ternes | January 24th, 2022 10:30 AM

Building an arcade machine has been a niche ambition of mine since highschool. I've played retro arcade games since my early childhood, with my top 3 games being Dig Dug, New Rally-X, and (of course) Galaga. So how could I get my hands on my very own machine?

Hardware Acquisition

The electronics involved in building an arcade machine is actually rather easy to come by nowadays. First we need a Raspberry Pi, which will be the computer working behind the scenes. I purchased a beginners Canakit from Amazon that contained a 32 GB SD card as well as all of the cords necessary to get the computer running. Next we need a monitor to display the graphics. I am using a Samsung monitor from the mid-2000s that I looted from my parent's stack of old electronic devices. This monitor has a VGA input, not HDMI, so I bought a VGA to HDMI cable. We also need a speaker system that can play the music and sound effects from our favorite 8-bit arcade games. Again, I returned to my parent's pile of electronics and found some '90s Harmon Kardon speakers which will fit right in with the retro look. Last, but not least, we need a controller input to communicate with the system. Now with RetroPie, there are many options an individual could choose from. The default that people use are XBOX-360 controllers, but that seems inappropriate to use with a retro arcade cabinet. So I went the extra mile and purchased an arcade DIY button kit from Amazon that contained all of the buttons I would want. There are many of these kits out there; this just so happens to be the one I went with.

Assembling

The first task to tackle is assembling the electronics. I plugged all of the cords in their respective places: HDMI to VGA cable in the HDMI port of the Pi and the VGA port of the monitor; the audio jack from the speakers to the audio port of the Pi; and the USB cables from the arcade button encoders into the USB ports of the Pi. The longest process was converting an old egg carton into a temporary holder for the buttons.

Installation and Configuration

Now comes the meat and potatos of the project. I won't go into extreme detail of each obstacle I hurdled, so if you want that, then check out this helpful YouTube video. First, I formatted the SD card and installed RetroPie from the official website. Then, I configured the audio port in the Pi so that the RetroPie library could connect to my speakers. Next I configured the controllers, troubleshooting along the way. Lastly, I added the ROMs from my PC by transferring them via FTP on my WiFi network.

Touch Ups

This is where it really comes together. I put some final touches on the menu UI by changing the theme, scraping some game art from arcade databases, and adding background music. And that just about does it for the electronics section of this arcade machine. Part 2 will cover the building of the casing to resemble a Bartop Arcade Machine design.
